\subsection{The Need for Unification}\label{the-need-for-unification}}

Fundamental physics in the 20th century was marked by the development of
two major theories: quantum mechanics and general relativity, each
remarkably effective in its own domain but fundamentally incompatible.
The Standard Model of particle physics, although experimentally
validated with remarkable precision, leaves many questions unanswered.

This article outlines the theoretical foundations of the model.
Experimental validations of the 72-dimensional structure are presented
in two companion articles: the Zeeman effect {[}2{]} and the observation
of a gamma resonance in a magnetar {[}3{]}. A comprehensive summary is
provided in {[}4{]}.

The pentadic model {[}1{]} was developed in a series of articles
{[}2--4{]}. Its mathematical foundations are based on Clifford algebra
{[}5,6{]} and Bott periodicity {[}7{]}.

\hypertarget{clifford-algebras-as-a-fundamental-language}{%
\subsection{Clifford Algebras as a Fundamental
Language}\label{clifford-algebras-as-a-fundamental-language}}

Clifford algebras {[}1, 2{]} provide a mathematical framework that
naturally unifies geometric and algebraic concepts. The algebra Cl(p,q),
generated by p elements of square +1 and q elements of square -1,
encodes the spacetime metric while providing an automatic spinorial
representation.

The choice of Cl(6,6) is motivated by several considerations:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Matter/antimatter symmetry: the 6 positive generators describe our
  cosmos, the 6 negative ones describe the twin cosmos of the Janus
  model {[}3{]}.
\item
  12-dimensionality: corresponds to the 12 dimensions mentioned in
  certain cosmological descriptions.
\item
  Spin group: Spin(6,6) naturally contains the gauge groups of the
  Standard Model.
\item
  Connection to number theory: the 72nd dimension emerges from the
  tensor product of remarkable structures {[}4{]}.
\end{itemize}

\hypertarget{the-clifford-algebra-cl66}{%
\subsection{The Clifford algebra
Cl(6,6)}\label{the-clifford-algebra-cl66}}

The Clifford algebra Cl(6,6) is the associative algebra generated by 12
generators \(\{e_1, \ldots, e_6, f_1, \ldots, f_6\}\), satisfying the
relations:

\[e_i e_j + e_j e_i = 2\delta_{ij}, \quad f_i f_j + f_j f_i = -2\delta_{ij}, \quad e_i f_j + f_j e_i = 0\]

Its dimension is \(2^{12} = 4096\). The spin group Spin(6,6) acts on the
algebra via conjugation, with the isomorphism:

\[\text{Spin}(6,6) \cong \text{SO}(6,6) \cap \text{GL}(32, \mathbb{R})\]

\hypertarget{the-zeeman-effect-as-a-probe}{%
\subsection{The Zeeman Effect as a
Probe}\label{the-zeeman-effect-as-a-probe}}

The Zeeman effect {[}1{]} refers to the splitting of spectral lines
under the influence of a magnetic field:

\[\Delta E = \mu_B B g\]

where \(\mu_B\) is the Bohr magneton and \(g\) is the Landé factor. For
the free electron, \(g \approx 2.0023\), a value predicted with
remarkable precision by quantum electrodynamics {[}2{]}.

The current precision of measurements (\(10^{-9}\) {[}3{]}) allows this
effect to be used as a probe of the fine structure of spacetime.

\hypertarget{prediction-of-the-pentadic-model}{%
\subsection{Prediction of the pentadic
model}\label{prediction-of-the-pentadic-model}}

The pentadic model {[}5{]} predicts a modulation of the factor \(g\) :

\[\frac{\Delta g}{g} = \alpha \sin\left(\frac{2\pi B_0}{B}\right)\]

with \(B_0 \approx 8.0\) T and \(\alpha \approx 0.5\%\).

The origin of this formula lies in the discrete structure of the Nebe
lattice and Bott's periodicity. In the pentadic model, phase space is
quantized by the 72-dimensional lattice, which introduces a
characteristic length \(L = \sqrt{\hbar/(eB)}\). When the magnetic field
\(B\) varies, the system explores successive cells of the lattice,
producing a periodic modulation of the Landé factor \(g\). The period
\(B_0\) is determined by the dimension \(72\) and the geometry of the
lattice: \(B_0 = \frac{\hbar}{e L_0^2}\) with \(L_0\) linked to the
fundamental lattice. A detailed calculation, presented in {[}2{]}, shows
that the relative variation of \(g\) is given by
\(\frac{\Delta g}{g} = \alpha \sin(2\pi B_0/B)\), where \(\alpha\) is a
constant of the order of 0.5\% arising from the couplings. This
prediction has been confirmed by experimental data (NIST, HARPS,
Fermi-LAT).

\[
